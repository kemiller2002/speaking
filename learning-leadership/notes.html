Conference Talk Speaker Notes (Acts I–VI)

Audience: software people in leadership · Format: conference · Length target: 45–60 minutes

Act I: The Shovel (≈7 minutes)

Goal: Establish credibility, humanity, and tension without explaining the thesis yet.

I want to start with a short story.

Years ago, one of my food companies operated in a small town where a single person owned a large percentage of the commercial buildings. Several restaurants, shops, warehouses. If something broke, eventually you ran into this person.

One morning, an HVAC technician showed up early to fix an air conditioning unit before the restaurant opened. He was looking for the building owner.

Instead, he found a guy in overalls sweeping the floor.

They talked for a bit. The technician explained what he was there to fix. The guy in overalls walked him to the unit, showed him what was wrong, answered questions.

Eventually the technician said, “I really need to talk to the owner before I start.”

And the guy said, “I am the owner.”

[pause]

That moment stuck with me.

Because sometimes leadership looks like strategy, roadmaps, and prioritization.

And sometimes leadership looks like sweeping the floor so the business can open on time.

[pause]

Most people in this room understand that instinctively. Software leaders especially. If a production issue is happening, you do not say “that’s not my job.” You jump in. You help. You pick up a shovel and start digging because the ditch is filling in and someone has to act.

That instinct is not the problem.

[pause]

The problem is what happens after.

[pause]

Because picking up a shovel for a moment is very different from being handed one forever.

And most organizational damage happens when no one notices the difference.

[pause]

I’m not here to talk about rigid roles, or job purity, or why people shouldn’t help. I believe in helping. I believe in learning. I believe in stepping outside your role when the situation demands it.

What I want to talk about is what happens when learning quietly becomes ownership. When help quietly becomes expectation. And when leadership fails not loudly, but silently.

[pause]

Act II: When Learning Becomes the Job (≈10 minutes)

Goal: Make the invisible pattern visible and recognizable.

In most software organizations, this starts with good intentions.

A product manager learns a little prototyping so they can communicate better with design.

An engineer learns more about customer context so they can make better tradeoffs.

A tech lead helps another team during a crunch because they understand the system.

More recently, AI makes all of this easier. Drafting, analysis, documentation, prototyping. The friction to help is lower than it has ever been.

None of this is bad.

[pause]

The failure mode is subtle.

The work repeats.
Other people start depending on it.
There is no end date.
Nothing else slows down.

At some point, learning quietly becomes the job.

[pause]

And the most dangerous part is this.

No one decided it.

There was no meeting. No handoff. No announcement that ownership had changed. It just happened because someone was capable and helpful and things were moving.

From the outside, everything looks fine. Work is getting done. Deadlines are met. There is no obvious crisis.

But something important has shifted.

[pause]

The organization has learned an unspoken rule. If something is unclear and important, someone will step in and carry it.

That rule feels efficient.

It is not.

[pause]

Because now ownership is no longer explicit. It flows toward the people who are most capable, most responsible, or least protected.

And if nothing else pauses, the work is already real. The organization just hasn’t admitted it yet.

[pause]

This is where role clarity arguments usually show up. Someone says, “That’s not the PM’s job,” or “That’s not engineering’s responsibility,” or “That shouldn’t be happening.”

They’re often right.

But by the time the argument starts, the damage is already underway.

[pause]

Act III: The Emotional Cost (≈8 minutes)

Goal: Make the cost human, not procedural.

Here’s the part that took me the longest to see.

The people who get quietly overloaded rarely complain.

[pause]

They don’t storm into meetings. They don’t escalate loudly. They don’t demand change.

Instead, something else happens.

They stop volunteering ideas.
They stop suggesting improvements.
They narrow their focus to exactly what is asked.

From the outside, it looks like stability.

From the inside, it’s withdrawal.

[pause]

And here’s what makes this particularly dangerous.

The people most affected are usually the most capable ones.

They were the ones who stepped in. They were the ones who learned the extra thing. They were the ones who picked up the shovel when the ditch needed digging.

Not because they were told to.

Because they cared.

[pause]

Over time, energy turns into caution.
Curiosity turns into compliance.
Ownership turns into obligation.

By the time someone finally says something, they are often already halfway out the door.

Leaders are surprised because nothing ever broke. Delivery never stopped. Metrics looked fine.

But silence is not sustainability.

[pause]

This is why systems that rely on goodwill fail the people they most depend on.

It is not that people stop caring. It is that caring without boundaries eventually costs too much.

[pause]

And this is the moment where most organizations think they have a people problem.

They don’t.

They have an ownership problem.

[pause]

Transition to Act IV

Transition line: So if good intentions are not enough, why does this keep happening in smart teams with experienced leaders?

Act IV: Why This Keeps Happening (Urgency, Heroics, and AI) (≈15 minutes)

Goal: Show this is structural, not a people problem. Explain why good teams still drift.

I think this keeps happening for three reasons that show up in almost every software organization.

The first is urgency.

Urgency is where role clarity quietly dies. Not because people stop caring about boundaries, but because urgency makes every exception feel reasonable.

We hear phrases like:
“We just need help this once.”
“It’s only for this release.”
“We’ll clean it up after.”

Under pressure, the question shifts from “who owns this” to “who can do it fastest.” And the people who can help usually do. That’s not a character flaw. It’s responsibility.

But here’s the problem.

The urgency passes. The exception remains. No one circles back to reset ownership.

What was temporary becomes expected. What was help becomes responsibility.

[pause]

The second is heroics.

Most organizations say they want accountability, but what they reward is heroics. Someone stays late. Someone jumps in outside their role. Someone saves the deadline.

That person gets praised. Sometimes they should.

But the system does not change. Ownership does not move. Expectations quietly reset.

The hero becomes the backstop.

Over time, accountability erodes because the organization learns a rule: if something is important and unclear, someone will save it.

Heroics solve today’s problem. Accountability prevents tomorrow’s.

[pause]

The third is AI.

AI did not create role creep. It just made it faster.

The moment a tool makes something easier, the question becomes “who can do this now,” not “who should own this.” Drafting, analysis, prototyping, documentation. The first step is cheap.

And because the first step is cheap, the long-term cost becomes invisible.

If AI output disappears tomorrow, who is accountable?

If the answer is “the person who happened to generate it,” that is not ownership. That is role creep at machine speed.

[pause]

Optional audience-check (keep it brief): “If you’ve seen any of these patterns, you’re not alone. This happens in strong orgs with good people.”

Act V: The Leadership Moment People Avoid (≈8 minutes)

Goal: Name the real failure: choosing comfort over clarity when nothing is on fire yet.

This brings us to the moment that decides whether this pattern becomes culture.

Something important is getting done, but ownership is unclear.

Someone capable steps in. The work moves forward. From the outside, it looks like leadership.

[pause]

The moment comes later, quietly.

Do we name that this work now needs an owner?
Do we pause something else to make room for it?
Do we disappoint someone by saying this cannot continue informally?

That is the moment most leaders skip. Not because they don’t see the problem, but because naming it creates friction.

It means choosing clarity over comfort.
It means risking short-term momentum.
It means saying no to something that is working.

So instead, we let it ride.

The work continues. The dependency deepens. The person carrying it absorbs more weight.

Nothing breaks immediately, which is why it is so dangerous.

[pause]

Leadership is not failing to act in a crisis. It is failing to act when nothing is obviously on fire yet.

By the time the cost is visible, the decision has already been made through inaction.

[pause]

Act VI: The Discipline That Holds (≈10 minutes)

Goal: Offer relief without turning the talk into a policy lecture. Give leaders usable guardrails.

So what do you do with this, as a leader in software?

I’m going to keep this simple, because complexity is how good intentions turn into process theater.

First, learning needs an end date.

Not “we’ll do it casually.” Not “sometime this quarter.” An end date. If learning goes long, you are no longer learning. You are staffing work without admitting it.

Second, repetition is the signal. If the work repeats, learning is over. At that point, name ownership.

Third, protect people from parallel learning overload.

One active learning effort per person at a time. If someone wants to add another, something else has to pause. If nothing pauses, the learning does not proceed.

Fourth, urgency creates exceptions. Exceptions require a reset. If you create an exception, assign someone to reset ownership when the urgency passes.

[pause]

Here’s the question that forces honesty.

What would we pause if this became real work?

If the honest answer is “nothing,” then the work is already real. You just haven’t admitted it yet.

This is not about control. These are protection mechanisms. They protect the people who always say yes. They protect your best operators from becoming invisible infrastructure.

[pause]

And I want to return to where we started.

Picking up a shovel is leadership. Sometimes you do work that is not your job because it needs to be done.

But picking up a shovel is not the same thing as being handed one forever.

Most orgs don’t fail because people won’t help. They fail because leaders don’t decide when help ends.

[pause]

Close gently: “Still thinking about how often we confuse momentum with sustainability.”
